You know the feeling.

That feeling when you're standing in front of the egg section at the super market. You want to use what little consumer power you have to buy good eggs. Happy eggs, where the chickens are free on the farm, aren't treated like abused laying machines, and the eggs are healthy and pure as nature made 'em.

You want the happy eggs.

But there are so many cartons of goddamn eggs. What even is a happy egg? And how do you choose that egg from this wall of cartons that seem to basically be all the same?

Egg labels can be read as a type of language, and like all language it has a series of rules. Some words are meaningful, and some are meaningless. Some terms are regulated by the Food and Drug Administration, and some mean literally nothing.

These rules can be used to clarify or manipulate what the text actually means. Data can help us learn to understand what the egg cartons are really trying to say.

So I went to three different supermarkets and surveyed every egg carton on the shelf.

Then I created an index of each of the label types that could appear on any given egg carton in the sample. Does the label list size, color, and grade of the egg? Check. Does it say the eggs are Kosher? Mark that. Does it say they are Free-Range, or does it not say anything at all about how the chickens who laid the eggs lived? Put it in a spreadsheet.

There's tons to read into an egg carton label, but I used my best judgement to pick the terms and concepts that came up the most frequently.

This visualization turns the text of egg cartons into a visual encoding.

The visual, eggsplained.

And here's how to read it.

First, each product gets its own set of concentric circles. Anytime the carton says something about a specific topic, I draw a circle. Words which have meanings more intrinsic to the egg are closer to the center of the circle.

As you go further out, the circles begin to address the welfare of the chicken that laid the egg - what that hen ate, where it lived, what medicines it took.

The outermost circles represent qualities of the packaing, like whether it says "fresh" anywhere or whethere there are extra tag lines.

Next, I color code each circle based on whether that word is good, bad, neutral, or is in fact misleading or is meant to persuade potential consumers.
