If you look at a map of the United States, the outlines reveal a secret about how we misinterpret landscape.

New Hampshire, Vermont, California, Arizona, and Indiana are carved out by the Connecticut, Colorado, and Ohio Rivers, respectively.

Most state boundaries are not delineated by a river, and are instead marked with straight lines. But this method leaves out an important truth: nature rarely follows straight lines.

When we need to divide land into sections for purposes of reference, government and stewardship, we could instead turn to hydrology. What if we used the flow of lakes, streams and rivers to define the landscape around us––and ourselves?

Meet your Hydrologic Unit

The USGS defines several watershed regions across the country, defined by their hydrologic unit code or HUC (pronounced "huck"). A watershed is a region where all the surface water is related, and each watershed has a code to identify its size and location in relation to all other watersheds nearby.

There's a lot of technical hydrology to explore, but this system of naming watershed boundaries is an important reminder that there are other ways of exploring who we are and where we are from.

Just as Earth was positioned in a particular pattern of relationship to other distant celestial bodies at the moment of your birth, the water around you was busy rising, falling, merging, forking, flowing, evaporating, and supporting life in the place where you were born. Here is a streamgraph of the total flow measured each day within each of the largest hydrologic regions of the US. Streamflow - which hydrologists call "discharge" and measure in cubic-feet-per-second - is a measurement of the volume of water passing a certain place at a certain time. Precipitation and weather events lead to greater flow as water from higher in the watershed flows down into rivers and then downstream.

The USGS has been measuring streamflow on thousands of rivers and bodies of water for decades through a process called streamgaging. The gages tapped for this dataset have been vetted by the USGS as good places to measure long-term hydrologic changes across the country.

By studying stage and flow data at gage sites around the country, we can get a holistic picture of how the water table changes over time and space. This is what the flow through the 21 HUCs across the United States looked like during the month surrounding your birthday.

Cultures throughout history have recognized and honored the connection between mankind and water. The Hawaiian people, for example, divide land into ahupua'as, adjacent watersheds flowing from mauka (hillside) to makai (seaside). These districts supported a wide variety of sustainable resource extraction and care bound by a common land ethic. If we seek to understand what truly connects us and develop sustainable practices for the next generation, we must listen to what water is telling us.

About the HUC Data

The map of HUCs is taken from the USGS National Map Viewer and aggregated at the HUC02 level, which is the "Region" level. Each HUC is subdivided further into HUC04 (larger regions), HUC06, HUC08, HUC10, and HUC12 (very small creeks) codes.

We used the Google Geocoding API to locate the GPS coordinates of your birth location, then used those coordinates to query the National Water Information System API to find out what HUC those coordinates were in.

About the Streamgraph Data

The streamgraph was generated with a subset of streamgages from the Hydro-Climatic Gage Network which vets streamgages based on their ability to measure long-term hydrologic and climatic impacts. Gages are selected by having at least 70 years worth of data up to 2009, and that their location is minimally impacted by human development.

We used your birthdate to generate a query to the National Water Information System API using that subset of gages that had daily values for the entire month surrounding your birth. We then summed the discharge values (in cf/s) by HUC to produce each band of the streamgraph.

Note that distribution of gages among HUCs, or even along river tributaries or mainstems, is NOT normalized or evenly distributed. This streamgraph is for interest, intrigue, and relative comparative purposes only, since hydrology is much more complicated than just summing discharge values.
